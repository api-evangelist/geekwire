<p>While much of the debate around artificial intelligence centers on the technology&#8217;s threat to jobs and society, one Seattle-area startup is turning that anxiety on its head — using AI not as a source of conflict, but as a tool to help resolve it.</p>



<p><a href="https://pilot.convexus.us/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Convexus</a> is a civic technology platform designed to help local governments, nonprofits, and community groups move past fragmented debate and toward coordinated action.</p>



<p>The startup was co-founded by <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/merrill-keating/">Merrill Keating</a>, a Bainbridge Island native and University of Washington graduate who was a <a href="https://www.geekwire.com/2021/junior-geek-month-uw-student-16-merrill-keating-leads-girls-along-stem-path/" rel="noreferrer noopener" target="_blank">Geekwire Junior Geek of the Month in 2021</a>, and her mother, <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/dkeating/">Doña Keating</a>, a veteran strategic advisor and consultant.</p>



<p>Unlike traditional social media that often amplifies division, Convexus &#8220;transforms dialog into action,&#8221; Merrill Keating told GeekWire. </p>



<p>&#8220;A lot of digital platforms only reward outrage and don&#8217;t really come to any kind of resolution,&#8221; she said. &#8220;They fragment across different types of tools, and there&#8217;s no real consensus anywhere.&#8221;</p>



<p>Convexus addresses the stalling point of modern civic engagement using what it calls an Explore-Align-Act framework —&nbsp;and AI-assisted facilitation — to help organizations identify shared goals and execute them.</p>



<p>&#8220;The AI has no agenda. It works fluently with pretty much anyone that wants to use it,&#8221; Doña Keating said, mentioning city councils, political candidates wanting to hear from constituents, book clubs, and corporate teams trying to solve a problem as examples.</p>



<p>The platform uses Anthropic&#8217;s Claude API to power its &#8220;Digital Facilitator&#8221; engine, surfacing areas of agreement, suggesting reframes when conversations turn heated, and guiding groups toward decisions. It also flags &#8220;bridge-building&#8221; contributions — the comments and perspectives that create connections across opposing viewpoints — rewarding constructive behavior and helping move groups from analysis paralysis to coordinated action.</p>

<p>Among the platform&#8217;s standout features is a live consensus meter — an animated visual dial showing the percentage of group agreement in real time — along with decision tracking that logs how many participants were involved and how long consensus took, what Doña Keating describes as an ROI story compared to traditional committee meetings.</p>



<p>The founders are quick to point out what sets Convexus apart from existing tools. </p>



<p>&#8220;Polis maps opinions, but it stops there,&#8221; Doña Keating said. &#8220;Change.org collects signatures, but it doesn&#8217;t have deliberation. Slack is for chat, but not decisions. No one integrates the structured dialog, plus the AI facilitation, plus the action outcomes at any scale.&#8221;</p>



<p>Convexus plans to monetize through a subscription model with tiers ranging from a free or low-cost option for grassroots groups to nonprofit, government, and enterprise plans. The founders are firm on one point: no ads, no data sales.</p>



<p>Convexus is currently inviting Pacific Northwest organizations to participate in free 30-to-90-day pilot programs to stress-test the platform. The startup was also selected as one of the first cohort of 10 high-potential startups at <a href="https://www.technology-alliance.com/upcoming-events/2026/3/3/seattle-investor-summitshowcase">Technology Alliance&#8217;s Seattle Investor Summit and Showcase</a>, set for June 3 at Microsoft&#8217;s Redmond campus.</p>



<p>For Merrill Keating, the platform is the natural extension of a lifetime of civic advocacy. </p>



<p>&#8220;As an advocate, a lot of what I&#8217;ve seen is people will just talk about things for years and years and years, and nothing ever gets done,&#8221; she said. &#8220;That is one of the most frustrating things as somebody who wants to actually see an impact being made on the communities I&#8217;m a part of. This is something that&#8217;s right up my alley.&#8221;</p>
