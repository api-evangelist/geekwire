<p>Women have long been left out of the datasets and decisions shaping everything from car safety to medical diagnoses. Industry leaders warn a rushed approach to artificial intelligence risks repeating those patterns.</p>



<p>That was a central message at this week’s <a href="https://www.womenintechregatta.com">Women in Tech Regatta</a> in Seattle, where speakers urged earlier and broader participation in AI development as adoption accelerates.</p>



<p>“Exclusion compounds over time and becomes much harder to detect,” <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/anya-edelstein/">Anya Edelstein</a>, learning experiences manager at Seattle-based <a href="https://www.highspot.com">Highspot</a>, said during an AI leadership panel on Wednesday. “If your perspective isn&#8217;t taken into account in the room when those decisions are initially made, it’s harder to make a change later down the road.”</p>



<p>Over the past few years, researchers have sought to mitigate the failures of machine-learning models trained on biased or skewed datasets, including <a href="https://news.mit.edu/2024/researchers-reduce-bias-ai-models-while-preserving-improving-accuracy-1211">misdiagnosis of kidney failure</a> in women. In the meantime, women worldwide are <a href="https://www.hbs.edu/ris/Publication%20Files/25023_52957d6c-0378-4796-99fa-aab684b3b2f8.pdf">about 20% less likely</a> than men to engage with AI tools, furthering the training disparity.</p>



<p>In the tech field, at least, the AI gender gap seems to be closing. It’s a noteworthy shift as companies race toward automation at scale, and concerns about misinformation and data security swirl around Anthropic and OpenAI <a href="https://www.geekwire.com/2026/filings-how-amazons-50b-openai-deal-actually-works-and-what-theyre-keeping-secret/">going public</a>.&nbsp;</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Women are leading AI strategy – with caution</strong></h5>



<p>Most women in senior roles (80%) are driving AI strategy in the workplace, where they <a href="https://chief.com/women-leaders-agentic-ai-study-2026/#key-findings">prioritize responsible adoption over speed</a>, according to a poll of more than 1,700 industry leaders published earlier this month by Chief, a women-focused leadership network.</p>



<p>This is often in contrast to company pressures to deploy AI tools and strategies at an increasingly rapid pace, said <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/mariamartin23/">Maria Martin,</a> product management director at Nordstrom. </p>



<p>“There&#8217;s less runway between a decision getting made, and a decision scaling,” Martin said at the panel Wednesday. “It’s important to get ahead and get involved early.”</p>



<p>In the group of women Chief surveyed, 71% were first at their companies to flag AI risks.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>



<p>“If we&#8217;re not <a href="https://carolinesinders.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/05/Feminist-Data-Set-Final-Draft-2020-0526.pdf">intentionally creating interventions</a> every step along the way,” said Edelstein, “bias has an opportunity to creep in.”</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Getting women into the room</strong></h5>



<p>The problem with bringing qualified women into AI leadership and decision-making spaces may start with hiring. At least two-thirds of recruiters use AI to screen candidates, a process shown to reproduce race and gender bias, often intersectionally.&nbsp;</p>

<p>In 2024, researchers at the University of Washington found that <a href="https://www.geekwire.com/2024/ai-overwhelmingly-prefers-white-and-male-job-candidates-in-new-test-of-resume-screening-bias/">AI resume screeners choose masculine names</a> over feminine 89% of the time, and white-associated names over Black-associated names 85% of the time. A year later, UW found that <a href="https://www.washington.edu/news/2025/11/10/people-mirror-ai-systems-hiring-biases-study-finds/">hiring managers mirror their AI model’s biases</a>.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Women and people of color face pressures to assimilate and code-switch – like using a race-and gender-neutral name on a resume – before they even enter the office. Once they’re hired, it’s about finding the right people for support, said <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/cynthiatee/">Cynthia Tee</a>, a longtime engineering leader and computer scientist.</p>



<p>Tee suggests more industry leaders can implement a sponsorship model, which requires greater intention, tangible risk and cost compared to typical allyship in the workplace.</p>



<p>“Keep insisting on promoting people who deserve it,” Tee said during a panel about navigating workplace dynamics. “Keep bringing more diverse people through your hiring pipelines. Keep bringing up people whose voices are not heard.”</p>



<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>The AI conversation is for everyone</strong></h5>



<p>There can be a confidence barrier to understanding or using AI, partially due to the industry’s “black box” design. <a href="https://www.linkedin.com/in/nandita-krishnan/">Nandita Krishnan</a>, a <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yo7xwDrFOSU">data scientist at Adobe</a> who builds apps on the side, suggests setting time aside every week to read up on the latest news and experiment with automating daily tasks. </p>



<p>“If you&#8217;re <a href="https://www.geekwire.com/2025/why-startups-should-pay-attention-to-vibe-coding-and-approach-with-caution/">vibe coding</a>, do it in a manner that makes the software still secure,” she said at the panel with Edelstein and Martin. “When you&#8217;re building out AI systems, it&#8217;s very prone to hallucinate. Add something to ground the LLMs, and give your agent this fact or database of knowledge to make sure it does not derail.”&nbsp;</p>



<p>Participation in AI decision-making isn’t limited to technical expertise. Edelstein suggests establishing values around AI – including education, healthcare and the environment – and finding industry leaders or companies who align to engage with.</p>



<p>Many workers are learning AI out of fear of being left behind, she added, but curiosity leads to better outcomes.&nbsp;</p>



<p>“If we can shift a lot of the perceptions around AI,” she said, “that is the first step to bringing more people into the conversation.”</p>
